    Operation Glowing Symphony

    In November, 2016 Operation Glowing Symphony targeted ISIS’s global media networks, aiming to disrupt its propaganda and communication infrastructure. By infiltrating ISIS’s network, USCYBERCOM gained control over key accounts, removed propaganda materials, and disabled ISIS’s ability to operate online.

    USCYBERCOM’s motivation for this operation was twofold: to undermine ISIS’s recruitment, propaganda, and operational abilities and to disrupt their communication channels, which directly supported ground operations and reduced ISIS’s influence.

    • Behavioral Analysis: Observing for unusual account activity and large-scale content removal, which may indicate the presence of USCYBERCOM's disruption efforts.

    The UK's support for allies in the Middle East, particularly in countering terrorism, may lead the NCF to engage in operations that indirectly or directly impact Lebanon. This could involve intelligence-sharing or cyber actions aimed at stopping terrorist activities that threaten regional stability. Here are some of the skills that UK Cyber Force specializes in:

  • Malware Development: Creating and deploying malware to critical system to disrupt infrastructure.
  • Intelligence Gathering-Information on Networks: The NCF conducts intelligence operations to get data on enemy networks, enabling them to expose certain key vulnerable points that could be used in future attacks.

    • Attack

    It was a collective cyber-attacking operation conducted by UK Cyber Force and other ally nations against ISIS to disrupt terrorist group operations running online propaganda and recruitment campaigns. The point of this operation was to restrict ISIS's levels of influence and its operations, which were flourishing in the digital space using different social media sites and websites to spread their extremist ideologies and recruit more people to encourage violence. The operation damaged the ability of the group to operate effectively through the targeted destruction and dismantling of ISIS' communication infrastructure. This is a general strategy to fight terrorism with innovation, underlining the place that cyber capabilities hold in national and international security efforts.


    Detect and Track

    Lebanon can detect and track the UK Cyber Force’s activities by using a combination of network monitoring, intelligence sharing, and proactive threat analysis. First, Lebanon should deploy advanced Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) and Intrusion Prevention Systems (IPS) to monitor its critical networks for suspicious behavior and detect known techniques. This includes identifying indicators of phishing attacks and social engineering tactics, which are common methods employed by the UK Cyber Force. Collaborating with regional and international partners through intelligence-sharing platforms would provide real-time threat data, helping Lebanon stay informed about potential cyber threats. To track their activities, Lebanon should use cyber threat attribution and forensic analysis in tracing suspicious activity to UK actors. Subscribing to cyber threat intelligence feeds and tracking open-source intelligence and dark web would be good means of tracking the planning or execution of UK operations. Partnering with private cybersecurity firms and forming dedicated incident response teams would allow Lebanon to continuously monitor for vulnerabilities and suspicious behavior, ensuring that the nation is prepared to identify and counteract any potential cyber threats.

    APT28 (Fancy Bear) Overview

    Affiliation

    APT28, also known as Fancy Bear, is closely tied to Russia's Main Intelligence Directorate (GRU). It operates as a key tool of the Russian military to gather intelligence, disrupt networks, and execute strategic objectives against NATO allies and other entities of geopolitical interest.

    Specialization & Capabilities

    APT28 focuses on cyber-espionage, targeting:

    • Government entities and military organizations (e.g., NATO allies)
    • Elections and political institutions (e.g., France, Germany, U.S. presidential elections)
    • Critical infrastructure sectors, including energy and transportation networks

    APT28 uses advanced phishing and spear-phishing campaigns, often tricking recipients into opening malicious attachments or clicking infected links. Once inside, it deploys malware to gather sensitive information over long periods.

    Some cyber tools attributed to APT28:

    • Sofacy malware: For credential theft.
    • X-Agent and X-Tunnel: Used for remote access and data exfiltration.
    • Drovorub: A rootkit for compromising Linux systems, especially those used in military applications.
    • VPNFilter malware: Targets routers and IoT devices to facilitate surveillance and disruption.

    Recent Attack Examples

    • U.S. 2016 Elections: APT28 targeted the Democratic National Committee (DNC) using phishing emails to steal sensitive campaign data, which was later leaked.
    • German Bundestag Hack (2015): Infiltrated German parliament systems, compromising data from key officials.
    • European Sports Organizations: Recent attacks on organizations like the World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) illustrate efforts to sow discord by exposing internal communications and records.

    Relevance to Slovakia

    APT28’s activities are particularly concerning for Slovakia because:

    • Political interference: Slovakia, as a NATO member, could be targeted during elections to destabilize alliances.
    • Espionage risk: Slovak government and military systems could be infiltrated for intelligence gathering on NATO activities.
    • Infrastructure disruption: Critical infrastructure, like energy grids or transportation systems, could be targeted during political tension or conflict.

    Detection & Tracking Strategy

    To counter APT28 activities, Slovakia can implement the following measures:

    • SOC monitoring tools: Employ SIEM solutions for advanced intrusion detection and monitoring.
    • Phishing detection: Simulate phishing attacks for training and use email scanning tools.
    • Threat intelligence platforms: Integrate threat feeds with indicators of compromise (IOCs).
    • Network segmentation: Isolate sensitive networks to limit malware spread.
    • Incident response coordination: Collaborate with NATO’s Cyber Rapid Reaction Teams and share intelligence with the NATO Cooperative Cyber Defence Centre of Excellence (CCDCOE) in Estonia.
