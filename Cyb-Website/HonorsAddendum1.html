Honors Addendum #1



The Law of War

Megan G. Howell
College of Engineering & Technology & The Honors College, Grand Canyon University
CYB-420HN: Global Perspectives on Cyber Warfare
Dr. Christine Bakke
November 10, 2024

Abstract

This report explores the Oxford Manual of 1880's four principles of warfare, which are military necessity, humanity, distinction, and proportionality. The principle of distinction is the most difficult legal issue facing contemporary combat. Through an examination of these principles and how they are applied in current conflicts, this paper makes the case that differentiation poses the biggest legal challenges because of the changing nature of warfare, urban combat situations, dual-use infrastructure, and technology developments.

Keywords: Oxford Manual 1880, laws of war, principle of distinction, military necessity, humanity, proportionality

The Oxford Manual of 1880

The Oxford Manual of 1880, often known as the "Manual on the Laws of War on Land," is an important document regarding the codification of international humanitarian law. It was created by the Institute of International Law with the goal of providing thorough guidance for military operations (International Committee of the Red Cross, 1880). The Oxford Manual discusses a number of essential notions that have grown into modern warfare principles. These can be understood as military necessity, humanism, differentiation, and proportionality.

Military Necessity

The Manual understands that the purpose of war is to weaken the enemy's military strength. Article 4 declares that "the laws of war do not recognize in belligerents an unlimited liberty as to the means of injuring the enemy" (International Committee of the Red Cross, 1880). This principle recognizes that certain activities are required in battle, but it limits what can be considered militarily necessary and recognizes the humanity of both parties, as described below.

Humanity

The principle of humanity runs throughout the Manual. Article 9 forbids the use of poison or poisoned weapons, indicating concern for unnecessary suffering (International Committee of the Red Cross, 1880). This approach seeks to reduce the harshness of conflict while protecting both fighters and civilians from unnecessary injury.

Distinction

The Manual highlights the need to distinguish between combatants and noncombatants. Article 7 declares that "it is forbidden to maltreat inoffensive populations" (International Committee of the Red Cross, 1880). This principle underpins modern policies for protecting people in violent situations. This is significant because it prevents innocent civilians from being abused by soldiers, as has happened in the past.

Proportionality

The manual also addresses the topic of proportionality. The destruction of enemy property is prohibited under Article 15 unless "imperatively demanded by the necessity of war" (International Committee of the Red Cross, 1880). This principle contends that military activities must be balanced against the risk of civilian injury, and that any civilian harm must be justified by a high possibility of catastrophic repercussions if the military does not engage in a specific situation.

The Primary Legal Challenge

Among these four criteria, differentiation presents the most pressing legal difficulty in modern battle. The Oxford Manual established this principle, which has become increasingly nuanced in recent years (Corn, 2019). The concept of distinction, a cornerstone of international humanitarian law, compels combatants to distinguish between civilian and military goals throughout armed conflicts. This strategy has proven more difficult to use in current conflicts, particularly in metropolitan areas where soldiers may face civilian populations (Schmitt & Schauss, 2019). The advent of new technologies in warfare has hampered the application of this principle, raising questions regarding the ability to distinguish between legitimate military goals and protected humans or objects (Ohlin, 2021).
